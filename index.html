A lesson redesigned around judgment—not memorization

BEFORE

The traditional lesson

The topic was commonly presented through a lecture, a slide presentation, definitions copied from the board, and an essay completed after the discussion. Learners could name technologies, but they had few chances to examine evidence, connect benefits with risks, receive immediate feedback, or consider who is affected.

LEARNING GAP

What learners need

Grade 7 learners encounter AI tools, connected devices, cloud services, online scams, misinformation, unequal access, and electronic waste in everyday life. They need a clear way to distinguish a developing trend from an emerging issue and to discuss both with evidence—not simply repeat a definition.

NOW

The digital redesign

ICT Pulse 7 combines short explanations, authentic scenarios, micro-assessments, instant feedback, collaborative evidence work, learner choice, and a public-facing performance task. The website is responsive, printable, keyboard-accessible, and usable as a low-bandwidth resource.

</>

Why GitHub Pages?

Full control, no heavy platform, one shareable link

GitHub Pages matches an ICT lesson because it publishes a lightweight, custom-coded learning site that works on phones and computers, supports built-in interactions without collecting learner data, and remains easy to update or reuse. A print mode provides an offline alternative.

STOP 01

Before learning · diagnostic

What is your current signal?

Answer five questions. This is low-stakes: the purpose is to reveal what to focus on.

1. Which statement best describes an ICT trend?
2. A free app secretly collects a learner’s location and contacts. What does this mainly illustrate?
3. Which pair correctly connects a trend with a possible issue?
4. Which situation is the clearest example of the digital divide?
5. Which action is most responsible when an AI tool gives a surprising “fact”?
STOP 02

During learning · concept builder

Separate the signal from the concern

A trend shows where technology is moving. An issue is a question, problem, or consequence that requires attention.

TREND

What is changing?

A direction that is emerging, spreading, or becoming more important in technology.

Clue wordsgrowing · connected · automated · adopted
↔
ISSUE

What needs attention?

A concern, challenge, debate, or effect involving people, systems, rights, or the environment.

Clue wordsrisk · access · fairness · safety · impact
4 EMERGING TRENDS

Select each card to reveal an example and a question.

T1
CREATE + PREDICT

Artificial Intelligence

+

AI systems can generate content, recognize patterns, and produce predictions or recommendations from data.

Everyday example: A writing assistant suggests revisions, while a photo app recognizes faces or objects.

Think: What should remain a human decision?

T2
SENSE + CONNECT

Internet of Things

+

IoT connects physical objects with sensors and networks so they can collect, exchange, and sometimes act on data.

Everyday example: A smart watch tracks steps; farm sensors measure soil moisture.

Think: What data does the device collect, and who receives it?

T3
STORE + ACCESS

Cloud Computing

+

Cloud services provide storage, software, or computing power through the internet instead of only on one local device.

Everyday example: A group edits one Google Doc from different devices.

Think: What happens when the connection is slow or the service is unavailable?

T4
FASTER + RESPONSIVE

5G Connectivity

+

Fifth-generation mobile networks are designed for higher speed, lower delay, and more connected devices than earlier generations.

Everyday example: More responsive video calls, remote monitoring, and connected transport systems.

Think: Who benefits first when new infrastructure is introduced?

4 EMERGING ISSUES

A trend can create benefits and still raise more than one issue.

I1
DATA + TRUST

Privacy & Cybersecurity

+

Personal data can be collected, leaked, stolen, or misused. Weak passwords, phishing, and unsafe sharing increase risk.

Response: Share less personal data, use unique passwords and multi-factor authentication, and verify unusual messages.

I2
TRUTH + SYNTHETIC MEDIA

Misinformation & Deepfakes

+

False or misleading content can spread quickly. AI can also produce convincing synthetic text, images, audio, and video.

Response: Pause, trace the original source, check the date and context, and compare with reliable reporting.

I3
ACCESS + OPPORTUNITY

Digital Divide

+

People do not have equal access to devices, affordable connectivity, accessible content, or the skills needed to benefit from technology.

Response: Provide offline options, shared-device workflows, accessible materials, and flexible deadlines.

I4
PLANET + HEALTH

Electronic Waste

+

Discarded electronics contain useful materials but can release hazardous substances when handled or recycled unsafely.

Response: Repair when practical, extend device life, donate responsibly, and use authorized e-waste channels.

MICRO-CHECK

Mainly a trend or mainly an issue?

Choose the best classification for each statement.

STOP 03

During learning · guided analysis

The consequence lab

For each situation, identify the response that protects learning, people, and evidence.

CASE 1 · AI + ACCURACYA classmate submits an AI-generated explanation saying 5G is “always available everywhere and has no risks.” What is the strongest next step?

CASE 2 · IOT + PRIVACYThe school considers smart ID cards that record when learners enter rooms. Which question should be answered before adoption?

CASE 3 · CLOUD + ACCESSA collaborative document is due tonight, but several group members have weak mobile signals. What is the most inclusive redesign?

CASE 4 · DEVICES + E-WASTEThe computer laboratory has unusable keyboards and cables. Which response is most responsible?

ADDIE DEVELOPMENT RECORD

How the innovation was built

AAnalysisUnderstand the barrier

Learners have mixed reading levels, device access, bandwidth, confidence, and prior exposure. The original lesson emphasizes recall and delayed feedback. The redesign therefore uses short chunks, authentic local contexts, immediate checks, and offline options.

DDesignPlan the learning evidence

Outcomes were written first. Activities follow Notice–Question–Decide–Create. Diagnostic, formative, and performance evidence are aligned before tools are selected.

DDevelopmentCreate accessible materials

The website uses semantic HTML, responsive CSS, lightweight JavaScript, concise media-like cards, print styles, keyboard controls, rubrics, answer rationales, and a responsible AI guide.

IImplementationDeliver in blended mode

The teacher projects the site or shares one link. Pairs may share a device; printable pages support offline work. Learners use built-in checks and selected Google Workspace tools when access permits.

EEvaluationUse evidence to revise

Compare diagnostic and formative results, rubric dimensions, completion data observed in class, learner feedback, and accessibility barriers. Revise unclear examples, pacing, and supports before reuse.

OBE

Backward alignment

Outcomes determine the activity and evidence. The impact brief directly measures discussion, analysis, evaluation, and creation.

TPACK

Three kinds of knowledge

Accurate ICT concepts meet case-based pedagogy and a platform that gives feedback and supports multiple formats.

SAMR

Meaningful task redesign

Instant feedback is Augmentation; shared evidence and peer comments are Modification; a publishable, revised brief for an authentic audience reaches Redefinition.

UDL

Barriers anticipated early

Concise text, visual structure, keyboard access, format choice, collaboration, print mode, and low-bandwidth use are built in from the start.

06 · AI integration and prompt guide

Use AI to extend thinking—not replace it

This activity is teacher-mediated. Learners must follow school policy, protect personal information, verify outputs, and acknowledge assistance.

Create six short Grade 7 scenarios about current ICT trends and issues in a Philippine school or community context. Include AI, IoT, cloud computing, connectivity, privacy, misinformation, access, and e-waste. For each scenario: (1) identify the trend or issue, (2) explain the classification in two sentences, and (3) suggest one current, reliable source to verify the claim. Do not invent statistics or citations. Use clear, age-appropriate language.
PURPOSE

Generate varied scenarios, questions, or formative feedback—not final learner work.

EXPECTED OUTPUT

Short, age-appropriate material with claims that can be independently checked.

TEACHER ROLE

Check accuracy, bias, reading level, curriculum alignment, safety, and source validity before use.

LEARNER ROLE

Question, compare, verify, revise, cite, and explain what human judgment added.

LIMITATION

AI may fabricate facts or sources, reproduce bias, miss local context, and sound certain when wrong.

THE 5-CHECK METHOD

Before an AI claim enters your work

  1. SSource

    Can you find an identifiable, reliable original source?

  2. DDate

    Is it current enough for a changing ICT topic?

  3. CCorroboration

    Does another trustworthy source agree?

  4. BBias

    Whose view or experience may be missing?

  5. RRelevance

    Does the evidence truly support your exact claim?

Age and safety note

UNESCO’s guidance calls for age-appropriate safeguards and identifies 13 as a minimum threshold for independent conversations with general-purpose generative AI tools. For younger learners, use teacher-prepared outputs or a school-approved, supervised environment. Never enter a learner’s full name, contact details, grades, health information, private messages, or account credentials.

TEACHER RESOURCEAnswer key and remediation guide+

Answer key

Diagnostic: 1-B, 2-C, 3-A, 4-D, 5-B.

Classification: 1-Trend, 2-Issue, 3-Trend, 4-Issue.

Consequence Lab: Case 1-C, Case 2-B, Case 3-D, Case 4-A.

Interpretation and response

  • 80–100%: Proceed to the performance task; invite deeper source comparison.
  • 60–79%: Revisit the trend–issue compare panel and complete one guided case with a partner.
  • Below 60%: Use the printed concept cards, teacher modeling, and two worked examples before retrying.

Learners may retry formative checks. The latest attempt is used for feedback, not punishment.

10 · Reflection

What changed in my thinking as a curriculum designer

01 · WHAT PROBLEM DID I WANT TO ADDRESS?

Knowing the names of technologies was not enough.

My starting concern was that a traditional discussion could easily become a list of terms for learners to copy and memorize. ICT trends change quickly, and the issues surrounding them are rarely simple. I wanted learners to move past “What is AI?” or “What is cloud computing?” and begin asking, “Who benefits? What could go wrong? What evidence supports this claim? What should we do?” That shift—from recall to judgment—became the center of the redesign.
